**Booking your flight and hotel separately can shave hundreds of pounds off a UK getaway — but only if you know the rules of the game.** Package holidays look effortless, and sometimes they genuinely are the cheapest option. More often, though, they bundle a tidy operator margin into a single tempting headline price, leaving you paying for someone else's convenience. With airfares broadly down on pre-pandemic levels, hotel inventory soaring on comparison sites like [JetMeAway](/hotels), and budget carriers flying from nearly every UK regional airport, the DIY traveller has never had more leverage. The catch is that "separate" only saves money when you book it cleverly — and stay protected when you do. Here's exactly how to play it in 2026.

### 1. The hidden margins behind those tempting package prices

Tour operators don't itemise. They quote you one figure for flights, hotel, transfers and luggage, then quietly mark up the bits you can't see. **TUI's combined airline-and-markets margin sat at just 1.5% in 2024, with a target of "more than 3%"** — but that's the headline. The real money is upstairs in TUI's hotel division, where bulk-bought beds at TUI Blue and Marella properties produced underlying earnings that jumped £91m year-on-year. Jet2 is even more upfront about it, telling investors that package holiday customers are "higher-margin per passenger" than flight-only travellers, and posting a **£593m pre-tax profit on £7.17bn revenue in FY2025** — an implied operating margin around 6%.

That margin doesn't appear from thin air. It comes from charter aircraft seats, bulk hotel allotments, and the simple fact that you can't price-check the components. Operators throw in 22kg luggage and transfers as standard precisely because most DIY comparisons forget to add them. Once you control for the bundle, the maths gets very different — particularly outside traditional summer beach trips.

### 2. Real numbers: what booking separately can actually save

Average European package prices have surged from **£1,038 per person for summer 2024 to £1,157 for summer 2025**, an 11% jump driven largely by hotel-cost inflation. Italy is up 7.4%, Portugal 6.8%, Cyprus 6.4%. Booking the components yourself lets you sidestep that operator-controlled price hike entirely.

Take a city break. A long weekend in Barcelona, [Lisbon](/destinations/lisbon) or [Berlin](/destinations/berlin) rarely shows up cheaper as a package because budget airlines flood the routes and hotel competition is fierce. A Friday-to-Monday stay built from a £45 Ryanair return and a £90-a-night central hotel will routinely undercut the equivalent operator package by **£100 to £250 per person** — and you'll get a hotel you actually chose rather than whatever the operator had cheap allocations on.

The same logic powers off-peak shoulder-season trips (April, May, late September, October), hand-luggage-only short breaks, multi-city itineraries and anything boutique. **The exception is traditional 7–14 night all-inclusive beach holidays in Spain, Greece, Turkey or the Canaries** — Which? found packages were cheaper on 8 out of 10 of those trips, with savings of up to 76% in extreme cases. We'll come back to that.

### 3. The freedom factor: flexibility you can't get with a package

Packages lock you in. Want to fly out Thursday and back Monday instead of the rigid 7-night Saturday-to-Saturday slot? Tough. Fancy switching from a beachfront resort on day three to a boutique stay in the old town for the second half of your trip? The package doesn't bend.

Booking separately means **you mix and match airports, dates and properties** like a traveller, not a customer being processed. Fly into Malaga, drive down to Tarifa, train it back from Seville. Stay two nights at a riad in [Marrakech](/destinations/marrakech), three at a beach hotel, two in a cool city pad. You can also cancel one element without losing the lot — change a refundable hotel without forfeiting your flight, or rebook a flight if a fare drops without abandoning your accommodation.

### 4. Cracking the flight code: timing, days and budget airlines

The booking-window rules have shifted. **Skyscanner's UK data points to 1–3 months ahead for European short-haul and 2–6 months for long-haul**, while Expedia's 2025 Air Hacks Report found international flights are surprisingly cheapest **18–29 days out**, saving up to 17% versus booking three months in advance. Whichever window you target, flexibility is the real saver: Skyscanner's "Whole Month" search routinely cuts another 30%, and the "Everywhere" tool lets you find the cheapest *destination* rather than the cheapest *flight*.

For day-of-week, **KAYAK puts Wednesday as the cheapest day to fly long-haul or domestically from the UK**, while Tuesday departures and Wednesday returns win for European hops. Sunday is the priciest day to fly home — adding around £93 to the average return — but, oddly, Sunday is the **cheapest day to actually book**, saving up to 17% on international fares. Forget the incognito-browser myth: Consumer Reports tested 372 searches and found prices were identical 88% of the time.

Budget airlines are the engine of cheap DIY travel, but Ryanair, easyJet and Wizz Air make their money on extras. A Which? investigation found Ryanair's advertised £12 cabin bag fee appeared just twice across 600+ flights, with an average actual fee of £20.50; easyJet's headline £5.99 figure never showed up at all in 500 tests, averaging £30. **Pre-pay your luggage online, check in at home, and bring a bag that genuinely fits the 40 × 20 × 25 cm under-seat dimensions.** Free flight-deal alert services like Jack's Flight Club and Secret Flying are worth signing up to — error fares from London routinely undercut packages by hundreds of pounds.

### 5. Hotel hacks: last-minute, direct booking and loyalty perks

Hotels work the opposite way to flights. **KAYAK's data shows booking around a week before your stay is typically cheapest**, and a NerdWallet study of 2,500 rates found booking 15 days out was 13% cheaper than booking four months ahead — and 22% cheaper at luxury properties. Apps like HotelTonight push that further, claiming savings of up to 27% with daily flash drops of 33% off. The exception is peak periods (school holidays, Christmas, big events) where you should still book three to six months out.

Once you've shortlisted on a comparison site, **check the hotel's own website**. Hilton Honors, Marriott Bonvoy, IHG One Rewards and Accor Live Limitless all offer member rates roughly 10% below public prices, plus free Wi-Fi, late checkout and breakfast that don't transfer if you book through a third party. OTA commissions run 15–25%, and hotels actively want to win you back direct. For UK leisure travellers, Hilton Honors gives the strongest return on free breakfast (especially via Amex Platinum's complimentary Gold status), while Accor's fixed-value points are easiest to make sense of for European trips. Tuesday check-ins and Thursday check-outs are typically cheapest; Friday arrivals can cost up to 20% more.

### 6. When a package genuinely makes sense

Be honest: packages aren't always the enemy. They tend to win on **traditional 7, 10 and 14-night all-inclusive beach holidays** to Spain, Greece, Turkey, the Canaries and the Caribbean, where operators bulk-contract food, drinks and entertainment that simply can't be replicated piecemeal. Family holidays with kids' clubs, child discounts and adjoining rooms are also natural [package territory](/packages).

The other reason to bundle is protection. **ATOL — the Civil Aviation Authority's safety net — only covers flight-inclusive [packages](/financial-protection).** If your operator collapses, you're repatriated or refunded, full stop. ABTA's 2025 Holiday Habits survey found 54% of UK travellers would actually pay more for that ABTA-protected booking, and 43% who chose a package said it was the best value option. For older travellers, big lump-sum bookings, or anyone who lost money in 2020, that peace of mind has a real price.

### 7. Stay covered: protecting your DIY trip

Booking separately means building your own safety net. Three layers do the job:

- **Pay by credit card** for any element over £100 to trigger Section 75 protection — your card issuer becomes jointly liable if the airline or hotel fails. Book direct with the supplier where possible to keep that link unbroken.
- **Buy [travel insurance](/insurance) the moment you book**, not the day you fly. Look for at least £5m medical cover, cancellation matching your trip cost, and crucially **Scheduled Airline Failure Insurance (SAFI) and End Supplier Failure cover** — Defaqto found 88% of annual policies don't include SAFI as standard. Post Office, Aviva and Staysure are consistently rated by Which?
- **Use an itinerary app** like TripIt or Wanderlog to consolidate confirmations into one place, with flight alerts and offline access. Forwarding emails to plans@tripit.com auto-builds the entire trip.

A GHIC card gets you state healthcare in the EU but won't repatriate you or refund a missed flight. Insurance still does the heavy lifting.

### The bottom line

Packages aren't dying — they still win on traditional all-inclusive sun trips and offer ATOL protection that DIY can't match. But for **city breaks, shoulder-season escapes, multi-stop adventures and almost anything outside the Saturday-to-Saturday template**, booking your flight and hotel separately routinely saves £100–£300 per person, gives you a hotel you actually picked, and keeps your itinerary genuinely yours. The skill isn't in being loyal to one approach — it's in pricing both, every time, and only paying the package premium when it earns its place.
